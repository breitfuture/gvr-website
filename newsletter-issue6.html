Tech Cracks, Iran Deal Wobbles, and the NOW Call Reminds Everyone Why Options Exist

Weekly Performance

The GVR portfolio declined -4.71% this week, roughly in line with a broad technology-led selloff that dragged the Nasdaq down -4.60% and the S&P 500 lower by -2.59%. For most of the week the equity book was tracking the market fairly closely — a collection of positions moving in roughly the direction you'd expect given the macro backdrop. What changed that picture dramatically was Friday, when ServiceNow surged more than 10% in a single session, sending the NOW October 2026 $100 call up $478, or +54.94%, in one day. That one afternoon is a useful illustration of what long options actually do: they sit quietly while the world sells off around them, and then they explode when the underlying finally catches a bid.

The NOW Call: A One-Day Lesson in Options Leverage

The ServiceNow October 2026 $100 call has been a source of pain for most of the past several weeks, sitting underwater as the software sector struggled and macro headwinds kept a lid on growth multiples. That changed on Friday. ServiceNow shares jumped more than 10% in a single session — the precise kind of sharp, catalyst-driven move that a long call is designed to capture. The position responded accordingly: the contract gained $478 in a single day, a move of +54.94%. To put that in context, a 10% move in the underlying stock produced a roughly 55% move in the option. That is delta and gamma doing exactly what they are supposed to do.

This is worth documenting honestly because the prior few weeks told the other side of the same story. Options decay over time when the underlying sits still, and they amplify losses when the underlying moves against you. Holding through that period of drawdown required conviction that the thesis — ServiceNow's position in enterprise AI workflow automation, contracted recurring revenue, and a long enough expiration runway — was intact even when the daily PnL said otherwise. Friday validated the patience, at least for one session. The position is not yet back to its peak value, and the thesis has not changed. The October expiration still provides meaningful runway, and the setup heading into the second half of the year — where enterprise software spending decisions tend to accelerate — remains intact.

The broader lesson is one worth internalizing: options positions can spend extended periods appearing to do nothing useful, and then compress weeks of potential movement into a single afternoon. Managing that emotionally — not exiting at the bottom, not over-allocating to chase the move — is most of the job.

Equity Book: Taking Profits and Buying Dips

The equity book saw meaningful activity this week on both sides of the ledger. On the exit side, CRDO (Credo Technology) was closed to lock in profits after a strong run that produced a total return of nearly +97% from cost — a clean example of the process working as intended, entering a setup with asymmetric upside and having the discipline to exit when the position had done its job. On the buy side, a number of new positions were initiated with a particular focus on AI-adjacent names and dip-buying opportunities in stocks that remain in strong long-term uptrends despite the current tech choppiness. New additions this week include LITE, AMZN, RDDT, SGML, NATR, PKX, JD, JYNT, LYB, CVE, NHYDY, and PBR — a deliberately broad set of entries spanning data infrastructure, energy, materials, and consumer names, reflecting a view that the current rotation out of mega-cap tech creates attractive entry points across multiple sectors for investors willing to be patient.

What Happened in Markets This Week

The week's defining dynamic was a sharp and broad rotation out of technology stocks, led by the semiconductor space, against a backdrop of renewed geopolitical uncertainty and mounting concerns about AI infrastructure spending sustainability. The trouble began Monday when South Korea's KOSPI index — heavily weighted toward chipmakers like Samsung and SK Hynix — triggered circuit breakers and fell nearly double digits in a single session, setting the tone for a global tech selloff that carried into U.S. markets on Tuesday. The SMH semiconductor ETF dropped roughly 7% on the day, with names like Micron, Nvidia, and Taiwan Semiconductor all falling sharply. The Nasdaq lost 2.21% on Tuesday alone as the contagion spread.

The catalyst behind the semiconductor sell-off was a Bank of America research note that flagged the possibility of up to three Federal Reserve rate hikes remaining in 2026 — a more aggressive path than markets had priced — alongside broader concerns that AI infrastructure spending may be running ahead of monetization timelines. Those concerns were amplified later in the week by a New York Times report that OpenAI is considering delaying its highly anticipated IPO to 2027, citing poor post-IPO performance from SpaceX and overall volatility in AI-related shares. For a market that has been pricing in an accelerating AI buildout as a key driver of semiconductor and software demand, the prospect of slowing capital formation in the space was enough to shake confidence meaningfully.

The Iran situation also deteriorated on Friday in a way that caught some investors off guard. President Trump took to Truth Social to announce that Iran had committed a "foolish violation" of the June ceasefire agreement by launching attack drones at commercial ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz. The accusation came just days after Iran had itself accused the U.S. of violating the agreement over continued Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon — a pattern of mutual blame that threatens to unravel the 60-day negotiating window that markets had priced as a meaningful de-escalation. Oil markets reacted with a roughly 3-4% decline in WTI and Brent crude on the day, which on the surface seems counterintuitive — but reflects the market's read that diplomatic escalation in words is different from a physical resumption of hostilities, and that tanker traffic through the Strait remains largely unimpeded for now. The more durable risk is that the ceasefire negotiations, which were supposed to address Iran's nuclear program and sanctions relief, are losing momentum before any of the hard issues get resolved.

There were some bright spots. The week's breadth was notably better than the headline numbers suggest: on multiple sessions where the S&P 500 and Nasdaq posted losses driven by a narrow band of mega-cap technology names, the majority of S&P 500 constituents actually closed in positive territory. Health care in particular had a strong week, with sector names like Moderna, Incyte, and several biotech names posting significant gains as investors rotated into areas less exposed to AI infrastructure spending concerns and rate hike risk. Micron Technology reported earnings after Wednesday's close that beat expectations, though shares sold off following the print — a classic "buy the rumor, sell the news" dynamic in a sector that had already priced in strong results after a parabolic run in 2026.

The week ended with the S&P 500 closing at 7,354.02, down roughly 2% for the period. The Dow Jones Industrial Average was the outlier, rising about 0.6% on the week as money rotated into more defensive and value-oriented names. The VIX closed at 18.41, elevated but not panicked — a market that is nervous but not broken. Next week brings a shortened trading schedule ahead of the July 4th holiday and a nonfarm payroll report that could meaningfully shift the rate narrative one way or another.
